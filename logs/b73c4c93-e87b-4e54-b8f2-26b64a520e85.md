## The documents writers actually have

From how writers and worldbuilders tend to work, their material falls into recognizably different *kinds*, and the differences that matter are about **authority and mutability**, not topic:

1. **The manuscript itself** — chapters, scenes. The only real "source of truth," yet constantly in flux. A weird property: something can be *in the manuscript* and still not be *decided* (a placeholder name, a scene that might be cut).

2. **Lore / worldbuilding notes** — geography, magic systems, factions, timelines, languages. Usually the messiest pile: settled decisions sitting shoulder-to-shoulder with abandoned ideas, in the same document, indistinguishable.

3. **Character documents** — sheets, arcs, relationships, voice notes. Special because they *change over story time*: "Elara distrusts the crown" may be true in Act 1 and false in Act 3. Facts here often need a *when*.

4. **Plot / structure documents** — outlines, beat sheets, foreshadowing ledgers ("gun placed ch. 3, must fire by ch. 20"). These are *promises*, not facts — a distinct kind of record: things the text has committed to that aren't yet fulfilled.

5. **Brainstorms and discards** — "what if" documents, cut scenes, old versions. Writers rarely delete; they exile. This pile is valuable precisely because it's untrusted — the graveyard of roads not taken.

6. **Style and voice notes** — tone rules, POV rules, "never say 'suddenly'." These aren't world-facts at all; they're *rules about the writing itself*.

## The shape I see in that

Three observations that cut across all six:

- **The tended/raw split isn't a folder split — it's line-by-line.** A single lore doc contains canon, maybes, and corpses. So the marble's core job may be less "put documents in the right bucket" and more "label statements wherever they live."
- **There are at least three kinds of trusted record, not one:** world-facts (true in the world), promises (setups owed a payoff), and rules (how the prose behaves). They fail differently — a violated fact is a contradiction, a broken promise is a dropped thread, a broken rule is a voice slip.
- **Time is a first-class field.** Worldbuilders need "true as of when" — both story-time (Act 2) and revision-time (as of draft 3).

The forbidden cell stays the same in all of it: *the narrator's guess filed as canon* — a model inference or draft idea quietly acquiring the authority of a decision.

## The five recurring complaints

**1. Silent canon rewrites.** The signature complaints: "My AI forgot that the character lost her arm in chapter 4," "ChatGPT changed the entire magic system between sessions," "gave my villain a completely different backstory halfway through." The pattern is that small inconsistencies creep in around chapter 5 — a name spelled differently, details slightly off — and by chapter 10 the beginning is effectively gone. Crucially, tools "rewrite factions and alliances on every chapter *without warning*" — the writer doesn't get told; they discover it later.

**2. Pasting the story bible doesn't work.** Writers already do the obvious fix — maintain a world bible and paste it into every session — and the widespread finding is that even then "the AI has no mechanism to *enforce* consistency; it optimizes for fluent prose over factual accuracy within the fiction." The bible is a suggestion, not a constraint.

**3. No home for finalized decisions.** One case study attributed a failed AI novel project to exactly the thing we identified: "with nowhere for finalized decisions to live, there was no way for AI *or the writer* to reference what was canon, what was evolving, and what was just throwaway." That's our forbidden cell described in the wild, independently.

**4. Voice drift is its own axis.** A writer of a 301K-word novel found that by chapter 80 the AI had lost the cadence of chapter 12, drifting into generic AI prose. The working fix people use is feeding *representative passages* back in each session and restating voice rules — meaning voice needs stored *exemplars*, not just rules. Style/voice really is a separate trusted record type.

**5. The maintenance burden kills existing tools.** Novelcrafter's Codex — the closest existing thing to a canon store — draws complaints not about the idea but about the *friction*: manual wiring, unclear setup, having to hand-select which scenes the AI reads. The manual's warning applies verbatim: "a safe path that's too heavy to use isn't fully safe."

## What this changes about the shape

Two additions to what we had, both straight from the complaints:

- **The gate needs a second direction.** We designed a gate for *writing into* canon. But complaint #1 is about output: the model contradicting canon in generated prose without flagging it. The marble can't stop a model from writing bad prose, but it *can* require a session-end continuity pass — "here's what I generated; here's each canon entry it touches; flag conflicts" — so contradictions surface at the desk, not in chapter 20. The complaint is never "AI made an error"; it's "*silently*."
- **The breath must be nearly free.** Complaint #5 says writers abandon anything that feels like data entry. The session-end confirm step ("here's what I think became canon — yes/no per line") has to be the *only* mandatory ritual, and it doubles as the intake form: the model fills the fields, the author just approves. Novelcrafter fails because the human does the wiring; here the model does the wiring and the human does only the deciding.

And one confirmation: voice/style exemplars earn a place in the tended region alongside world-facts and promises — three record types, as we suspected.

So the emergent bucket shape, derived from complaints rather than taxonomy: **canon facts** (with story-time), **promises/setups**, **voice** (rules + confirmed exemplar passages), **raw inbox** (labeled by origin), and an **audit/continuity log** where both gate refusals and detected contradictions land. Nothing else until real friction demands it.
