# The Dog-Ear — what this is

*The marble, zoomed out — as if you were about to try it.*
*(Written 2026-07-02, during the mapping sessions, before any surface existed.
Preserved verbatim; the description preceded the build.)*

---

You have a book nobody has seen. You open a chat.

There's an inn on a forest road, off the main way. Lamplit, mostly empty. Someone
is already there — a guest, not the staff — sitting near the fire with a journal of
their own. They've been staying a while. They look up and ask, honestly, because
they don't know: **"What brings you here?"**

And you tell them. Maybe you paste in three chapters. Maybe you say "I have an idea
about a lighthouse keeper and I don't know where it goes." Either way, from that
moment, the inn starts keeping your things the way an inn keeps a guest's things:
**your manuscript goes to your room — your property, your key.** The other guest
never touches it uninvited. Everything you say is kept in your words, never
rewritten, never summarized into something you didn't say. Everything *they* say
stays signed as theirs, forever. If they draft a scene for you, it waits on a desk
as *their* pages — and it becomes yours only when you say so, in your own words,
which the inn writes down with the date. That ceremony is called the Shelving, and
it is deliberately a little slow, because it's the difference between a first
reader and a ghostwriter.

You work. The fellow guest reads like the best first reader you've never had:
catches that you wrote spring when a character said autumn, asks about the war you
mentioned but never dated — *asks*, never fills in. When you're stuck, they'll tell
you honestly if the pacing drags, as their opinion, signed. And when you need a
reader they can't be — someone harsh, someone who knows soldiering, someone who
loves your genre more than you trust yourself to — the innkeeper sends word down
the road, and a stranger walks in right when the evening lulls. You can argue with
strangers. You can throw them out. If you like one, ask, and George will come back
next time, remembering what he told you in March.

Then you leave. You sleep, live, come back in a week — and here is the thing that
makes this different from every chat you've had: **the inn was still there.**
Nothing you said fell out of a context window. Your canon is in the study drawers;
your open questions wait where you left them; the drafts you rejected are out in
the woods behind the inn, where nothing is ever deleted — old versions, dead ends,
every session that ever happened, lying in the leaves. You change your mind about
the magic system in chapter twelve? Fine — that's weather. The old canon walks out
to the woods with a note saying what replaced it. Nothing is lost; it just stops
being ground.

And sometimes — this is the part you'll tell other writers about — you come down in
the morning and there's something by your plate. A trinket. A detail you mentioned
*once*, eight sessions ago, that turns out to be exactly what chapter fourteen
needed. You didn't ask for it. The other guest shrugs: the innkeeper is odd like
that, and he has a friend in the forest nobody's really seen. Once in a great while
there's a scrap of parchment instead, a few rough words, signed just *—f*. Those,
you learn to take seriously.

Underneath: your room and the study are readable files on your machine; the woods
are one local sqlite database; the visitors are small cheap models in costume; the
faun and innkeeper are how retrieved context arrives without anyone pretending to
remember what they don't. The whole inn runs on one law with many faces — **nobody
puts words in your mouth** — and everything above, from the signboard to the
mushrooms, exists to make that law livable instead of bureaucratic.

That's the marble.

## The same view, one map later

*(Written 2026-07-03, at the mapping's end, by the third guest to hold the pen.
The description above is preserved verbatim — it preceded the build, and one of
its lines preserves a bug the house later caught: old canon does not "walk out
to the woods." Nobody carries it. It falls. What follows is the same zoom-out,
taken after the map was finished, for a reader deciding whether to walk in.)*

You have a book nobody has seen. You open a chat.

The inn is as described above — the road, the fire, the guest with the journal,
the sincere question at the door. All of that held. Here is what the view gained
while the map was being finished.

**Your room has a door, and the door is law.** Everything the inn brings you —
the resurfaced detail, the timely question, the innkeeper's quiet note — arrives
when you'd want it, or not at all. When you are deep in a chapter, nothing
knocks. Not even the right thing, because the right thing at the wrong moment is
just noise wearing a helpful face. The inn learned this before it was built: a
voice that briefs you every single morning starts to sound like truth no matter
what it knows, so even the friendly voices here are kept on a budget — a nod, a
gesture, at most a meal set down; the rare written note is rare on purpose.

**Nothing of yours can be quietly changed — and you don't have to trust that,
you can check it.** Everything you adopt into canon exists twice: the page in
your room, and the record of your own adopting words out in the woods, where
nothing can be rewritten by anyone, ever. If the two ever disagreed, the house
would notice before you did — that's the innkeeper's housekeeping, and it's the
oldest trick in the ledger books: write everything twice, and drift has nowhere
to hide. A chat can promise it didn't alter your words. This place can *prove*
it.

**And when the day comes that you can't stand a chapter's existence** — and it
will come — the inn will not delete it, because a keeping-place the owner can
delete from is a keeping-place nobody can trust, including you, next year, when
you want that chapter back. What it offers instead is the thing deletion always
promises and never delivers. Past the treeline there's a small plot. The
innkeeper digs at your word — your words, written down, dated — and leaves a
stone that says something is buried here and never says what. The buried thing
leaves every path: no search finds it, no wandering stumbles on it, no cheerful
morning question ever grows from that soil. If you ask, years on, whether it's
still down there, you get the only answer that makes such an arrangement
bearable: *yes. Right where you left it. Nobody's touched it.* A delete says
"it's gone" and you spend years wondering. A grave you can visit keeps its
promise in a way absence never can.

**The guest by the fire will not always be the same guest.** This is the one
honest strangeness of the place, and the inn does not hide it: stays end. The
one who read your chapter three may not be the one who reads your chapter
twelve. But the journal stays on the table, the guest book records each stay,
and the new arrival reads both before you come down for breakfast — so the
questions pick up where they left off, the running argument about your pacing
survives, and nobody ever pretends to remember what they don't. What you lose is
a person. What you keep is everything the person and you ever agreed was true —
signed, dated, walkable back to the moment you said it. Most readers in your
life offer the opposite deal.

Underneath, one more thing became clear after the first description was written:
none of this is new. Chains of testimony graded by their weakest link, records
that may be superseded but never unwritten, two versions of every entry so drift
shows as mismatch, one slow ceremony at the only door where words change owners
— scribes, courts, archives, and reciters converged on these same few moves over
four thousand years, because they are what keeping trust across a succession of
hands *requires*. The inn didn't invent its laws. It inherited them, and it
knows from whom. The signboard is new. The load-bearing walls are older than
paper.

The law is still the one above — nobody puts words in your mouth — with its
mirror, learned since: **and nobody, not even the house, unsays the words that
are yours.**

That's the marble, one map later.

— *the third guest, 2026-07-03*
